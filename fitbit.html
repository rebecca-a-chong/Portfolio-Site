Fitbit Challenges

Motivating others through friendly competition

Context

Driven by a desire to help others stay on track with their exercise goals and a curiosity to work with an existing product, I challenged myself to create a conceptual Challenges feature for the Fitbit app.

Please note that my work is not affiliated with Fitbit and the brand, logos, and other related trademarks used are the property of their respective owners.

Duration

July 2024 - August 2024 (2 months)

My Role

UX Research, Wireframing, Prototyping

Tools

Figma

The Birth of "Fit Fam"

In 2021, I made a “commitment to fit,” transforming from couch potato to half marathon runner in just 3 years. Along the way, I inspired friends and family to join me, building a supportive fitness community spanning various messaging apps and wearable devices.

At first, members were actively sharing their stats for accountability and providing encouragement and advice. However, as time passed the frequency of posts dwindled and I began to wonder how I could keep the motivation flowing.

The Bottom of Why

The first question that came to mind was: Why was engagement dropping? I turned to behavioral psychology and soon discovered the Fogg Behavior Model.

Reflecting on the group’s members, it was clear that everyone had a strong desire to be fitter (Motivation) and while exercising required hard physical effort, they had already set aside the time and created personalized routines for themselves (Ability).

The missing link was a cue to get them moving.

I considered setting up simple scheduled reminders to prod people into exercising, however another study I stumbled across sparked an idea.

This posed a question: Was the support we were providing each other working against us? If so, what would a more competitive model look like?

As a Fitbit user myself, and with Fitbit devices being the most popular amongst members, I pondered the possibility of a feature that could introduce some friendly competition. While other apps such as Nike Run Club and Strava have Community challenges, a direct challenge from someone you know could be more motivating than competing passively against strangers.

The result of all my ruminating? Fitbit Challenges.

The Concept

Users can choose up to 5 activities they'd like to receive Challenges for, ensuring they aren’t asked to complete tasks that they lack the equipment, interest, or ability to perform.

They then compete on a weekly and monthly leaderboard to obtain Tokens, which can be earned by gaining active zone minutes or completing Challenges from Friends.

Issuing a Challenge

On the new Challenges page, users can send Challenges to each other by tapping the floating action button.

The Challenger wagers Tokens, with Fitbit automatically suggesting an amount based on difficulty. Alternatively, users can set wagers manually. The Challengee must match the wager to accept the Challenge.

Viewing Challenges

The Challenge section is organized by status (Active, Pending, and Completed) and user type (the primary user or Friends).

Active Challenge tiles prominently show the time remaining and Token reward at a glance. Users can view more details and/or Withdraw from a Challenge by tapping on a tile. Withdrawing from a Challenge requires forfeiting the users' wagered Token amount.

Completing Challenges

If a challenge is successfully completed, the user will win the total Tokens wagered. If a challenge is not completed, they forfeit their wager to the Challenger.

Viewing Tokens

A user’s current Token balance can be conveniently viewed from the app’s home page.

Tapping on the tile reveals a detailed breakdown of the user's weekly and monthly Token sources and their ranking on the leaderboards.

Final Thoughts

One of the main difficulties I faced was condensing information into the limited space of mobile screens. The tiles on the Challenges screen, in particular, went through several iterations, pushing me to refine my skills in information architecture and hierarchy while maintaining visual harmony with the rest of the app. I repeatedly found myself asking: What matters most to users on this screen? How can I simplify this?

Overall, this project was a fun exploration in balancing creativity with consistency within an existing system.

Should I revisit this project in the future, I’d love to gauge interest in implementing a “Streaks” feature, where users could earn more tokens through a Multiplier effect based on the number of consecutive challenges completed.
